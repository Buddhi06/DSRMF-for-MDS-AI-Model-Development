Task 05: - Identify the risk model and analytical approach

    Next

Organisation define the risk model for use in conducting risk assessment and the specific analytical approach to be used for the risk assessment including:

  • Assessment approach
  • analysis approach

Risk Model
Risk model defines the risk factors to be assessed and the relationship among those factors [2].
“The framework uses threat, vulnerability, impact and likelihood as the risk factors.

Analytical Approach
Selecting the analytical approach includes: selecting the assessment approach and selecting the analysis approach

Select the Risk Assessment Approach

The risk is determined by considering the likelihood that known threats exploit known vulnerabilities and the resulting consequences or adverse impacts if such exploitations occur. The risk assessment phase starts with selecting the risk assessment approach. There are three main risk assessment approaches: qualitative, quantitative and semi-quantitative.


The qualitative approach typically uses a set of methods, principles, or rules for assessing risks based on nonnumerical categories or levels (e.g., very low, low, moderate, high, very high) [2]. However, in most circumstances, the range of values in qualitative approach is comparatively small, which makes the relative prioritisation or comparison within the set of reported risks difficult [2]. Furthermore, the assessment results could depend on the individual experiences if each value is not very clearly defined or is not characterised by meaningful examples [2]. Therefore, the levels of qualitative scales should be unambiguous, should not overlap, the qualitative descriptions should be expressed in objective language and their increments should be clearly defined [4].

The quantitative approach typically uses a set of methods, principles or rules for assessing risk based on use of numbers where the meanings and proportionality of values are maintained inside and outside the context of the assessment [2]. This type of risk assessment most effectively supports cost-benefit analysis of alternative risk responses or courses of action [2]. The risk is usually expressed in monetary values which shows the amount of money an organisation may lose over a certain period of time if the threat event occurs or vulnerability is exploited. Risk level can be calculated using any method and taking into account any relevant factors, but it is usually shown by multiplying the likelihood by the consequence [4]. Likelihood represents the probability or frequency of an event occurring within a given timeframe, i.e., annually (per year), large (e.g., per century) or little (e.g., per second) [4]. However, the meaning of the quantitative results may not always be clear and may require interpretation and explanation, i.e., usually to explain the assumptions and constraints on using the results [2]. Usually, the risks can be quantified as annual loss expectancy [4] and can be calculated by using the formula: ALE=SLE×ARO SLE=AV×EF ALE-Annual Loss Expectancy SLE-Single Loss Expectancy=The value of single data security threat ARO-Annual Rate of Occurrence=Estimated frequency with which that data security threat might occur in one year AV-Asset Value = The total monetary value of the asset EF-Exposure Factor = The percentage of loss likely to occur if the threat event occurs

The semi-quantitative approach typically uses a set of methods, principles, or rules for assessing risk that uses bins, scales or representative numbers whose values and meanings are not maintained in other contexts [2]. Semi-quantitative approach can provide the benefits of both quantitative and qualitative approaches. The bins (e.g., 0-15, 16-35, 36-70, 71-85, 86-100) or scales (e.g., 1-10) can be easily translated into qualitative terms that support the communication of risks. E.g., a score of 95 can be interpreted as very high, while also allowing relative comparisons between values in different bins even within the same bin (e.g., the difference between a risk with the score of 70 and 71 respectively is relatively insignificant, while the difference between risks scored 36 and 70 is relatively significant) [2]. In semi-quantitative approach, the role of expert judgement in assigning values is more evident than in a purely quantitative approach [2].

Comparison of the three risk assessment approaches



Approach Advantages Disadvantages
Qualitative Approach
  • Quick to implement as it does not rely on numerical data.
  • No special tools or software required.
  • Less expensive than the quantitative approach.
  • Can assess intangible assets (e.g., reputation, goodwill).
  • Involves non-security and non-technical staff easily.
  • Helps in early identification and prioritization of risks.
  • Results depend on expert experience and team quality.
  • Subjective and may introduce biases.
  • Difficult to compare risks due to a small range of values.
  • Limited ability to quantify and compare risks accurately.
Quantitative Approach
  • Provides objective, measurable risk assessment.
  • Enables comparison of risk severity.
  • Well-understood asset valuation and risk mitigation options.
  • Supports cost/benefit assessments, aiding senior management decisions.
  • Expresses results in management-friendly terms (e.g., monetary value, probability).
  • More time-consuming and complex.
  • Data collection can be costly and difficult.
  • Requires interpretation and explanation of assumptions.
  • Needs a significant amount of historic data and expertise.
  • Relies on available data quality.
  • Requires specialized methods/tools (e.g., EMV, Decision Tree Analysis, Monte Carlo Analysis, FTA, FMEA).
Semi-Quantitative Approach
  • Incorporates expert judgment in value assignments.
  • Provides better relative prioritization than qualitative approaches (if scales offer enough granularity).
  • Does not require specialized tools.
  • Requires clearly defined scales or value bins.
  • More time-consuming than a qualitative approach.

The framework proposes to use the following approach

Most security risk assessment models use qualitative or semi-quantitative approaches to assess security risks [1]. Quantitative risk assessment has the following gaps when considering for data security risk assessment of MDS AI model development:

  • Lack of sufficient historic data - As the quantitative risk assessment utilises historical data to determine the likelihood of a threat event occurring and or vulnerability being exploited, the historical data that is not recently updated can cause errors in the risk assessment. Moreover, as data security of MDS AI model development is an emerging domain, there is a limited data to estimate the probability and impact numerically.
  • Complexity and resource constraints – As quantitative risk assessment methods require extensive resources, expertise and tools for assessing risks, the small MDS development organisations with limited security experts may not have the capability to conduct the assessment.
  • Difficulty in communicating and understanding the results – As Quantitative risk assessment use complex formula (e.g.; Annual Loss expectancy), interpreting the results is difficult and time consuming. Qualitative and semi-quantitative assessment use risk matrices and verbal risk levels (Eg: Low, Medium, High) that are easier to interpret makes the assessment more convenient.

Therefore, this framework suggests using qualitative and/or semi-quantitative approaches for data security risk assessment.

Select the Risk Analysis Approach

There are three applicable risk analysis approaches depending on which risk factor (threat, vulnerability and impact) is assessed first. Threat-oriented, asset/impact-oriented and vulnerability-oriented [1]. The choice of which approach to follow should be documented in the security risk management plan [1].

The Threat-oriented approach starts with the identification of threat sources and threat events, and focuses on the development of threat scenarios [1], [2]. Vulnerabilities are identified in the context of threats and the impacts are identified based on adversary intent [2].
The Asset/impact-oriented approach starts with the identification of impacts or consequences or concerns and critical assets, and identifying threat events that could lead to and/or threat sources that could seek those impact or consequences [2]. There you need to perform a complete asset inventory and assess the potential impact if those assets are compromised. Then, identify vulnerabilities that could lead to those impacts and threat sources/events that might initiate exploits utilising those vulnerabilities [1].
The Vulnerability-oriented approach starts with a set of exploitable weaknesses/deficiencies or predisposing conditions and identifies threat events that cold exercise those vulnerabilities together with possible consequences of vulnerabilities being exercised [2].

Each analysis approach takes into consideration the same risk factor (threat, vulnerability and impact) and contains the same set of risk assessment activities but in different order [2]. It is up to the manufacturer to decide which strategy to use, and the decision should be documented in the security risk management plan [1]. AAMI TIR 57 standard suggests to use the three approaches based on the following requirements:

  1. If the target of the analysis is a complete system or if there are specific threats that should be addressed – Threat-oriented approach
  2. If the target of the analysis is a single device that will be used in the context of a larger system – Asset/impact-oriented approach
  3. During post-market deployment, i.e., when new risks may be learned through the exposure of new vulnerabilities – Vulnerability-oriented approach

The framework uses the asset/impact-oriented risk analysis approach. It is based on the STRIDE threat modelling, i.e., an asset-centred threat modelling tool which is identified as a promising starting point for AI threat modelling by independent agencies like the European Union Agency for Cybersecurity (ENISA) .
